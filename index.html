Hi, I'm Sebastian. I'm a Master's student at Georgetown building tools to look inside neural networks, and using them to ask whether ethical reasoning can be represented inside a model rather than just trained into its behavior.

Before this I did a mechatronics and production engineering degree in Mexico City, then spent about four years in data and ML roles — first at Scale AI, where I built evaluation frameworks for NVIDIA's RLHF alignment work (GPT-4 as judge, the HelpSteer2 methodology), then at Welo Data, building ML pipelines for annotation quality and operations. Watching how much of "AI safety" in practice comes down to a reward model and a rubric is part of why I ended up here — training a model not to do something isn't the same as the model understanding why not to.

I built ModelLens, an interpretability toolkit that works across architectures rather than just transformers, and I'm using it to study Stoic LLM Steering — a project trying to push a model's outputs toward Stoic philosophy and then checking, mechanistically, what that steering actually changes inside the model versus what it just makes the model sound like.

Research focus

The question behind the two projects below

Most AI safety right now is behavioral. RLHF and red-teaming teach a model what gets penalized, not why something is harmful — which is part of why safety breaks under temperature changes, adversarial prompts, or just enough distribution shift. There's no underlying principle being learned, just pattern-matching on what got rewarded.

What I'm actually working on is whether that has to be the case — whether something like Stoic ethics (the dichotomy of control, temperance, virtue) can be encoded at the representation level instead, so safety is mechanistically grounded rather than just trained in. I built ModelLens to look inside a model and Stoic LLM Steering to actually try pushing one toward that framework, then used the first to check what the second is really doing.

The honest answer so far is complicated, and that's kind of the point — steering shifts how a model talks more reliably than what it decides, which tells you something real about where the intervention does and doesn't work. Everything here runs on a laptop with 1B–3B models, which limits what I can claim, but it's also forced the evaluation to be more careful than it might have been otherwise.

ModelLens

Same behavior, different circuits

Repository ↗

An interpretability toolkit that works on whatever architecture you hand it — transformer, CNN, LSTM, MLP — instead of assuming transformers like most tools in this space do.

from modellens import ModelLens lens = ModelLens(model) # auto-detects the architecture results = lens.layer_probe(inputs) # generalized logit lens attn = lens.attention_map(inputs) effects = lens.activation_patch(clean, corrupted) # causal intervention

I used it to compare two different Stoic-steering methods (below) at the circuit level: they produce the same Stoic-flavored text, but activation steering runs through a single mid-layer bottleneck while LoRA rewires the model's output layer. Same behavior, genuinely different mechanism.

Stoic LLM Steering

What steering actually moves

Repository ↗

The core question: if you push a model's outputs toward Stoic philosophy, does that change what the model decides, or just how it talks? I built a full pipeline — contrastive pair generation, activation steering (CAA), LoRA fine-tuning, and judge-free forced-choice evals — to find out.

Short version: style moves easily, content moves with clean data, and decisions mostly don't. Activation steering never shifted the model's actual choices, even at strengths that broke coherence. LoRA fine-tuning did reach the decision layer, but what it installed wasn't clean Stoic reasoning so much as a passivity bias for one philosopher and an unresolved confound for another.
